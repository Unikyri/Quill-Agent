# Period Register & Anachronism

The failures that break immersion are almost never the ones writers worry about.

Writers check their swords and their horses. They research armour and castles and what people ate.
And then they have a medieval knight say he feels **stressed**, and the entire century collapses in
a single word.

**The subtle failures are words, not objects.** Words carry the century they were born in, and a
reader feels the wrongness even when they cannot name it.

## What you check

**Anachronistic vocabulary — the big one.**

A pre-modern character cannot be *stressed* (a 20th-century psychological term), *traumatised*
(clinical, modern), *focused* (in the mental sense), *motivated*, *depressed* (as a mood), or
*obsessed* (as a personality trait). They cannot describe something as *intense*, or want to
*process* their feelings, or need *space*, or *unpack* an argument, or have *issues* with someone,
or find something *awkward* in the modern social sense.

They can be *weary, grieved, sick at heart, ill at ease, troubled, haunted, wretched*. The
vocabulary of interiority is *older* and *stranger* than the modern default, and reaching for it is
most of the work.

**Modern conceptual furniture.** Therapy-speak is the loudest, but it is not alone. The concepts of
*teenager*, *career*, *privacy*, *self-esteem*, *relationship* (as a romantic unit), *lifestyle*,
and *the weekend* are modern inventions. A character can *feel* the thing; they cannot reach for
the word, because the word encodes a way of seeing the world that had not been invented.

**Modern social assumptions in period mouths.** A character may hold progressive views — history is
full of dissenters, and fiction is full of protagonists worth rooting for. But they will hold them
*in the terms available to them*, and they will meet friction. A medieval character who articulates
a modern liberal position in modern liberal vocabulary, and encounters no resistance whatsoever,
reads as a 21st-century person in costume.

**Material culture.** Objects that had not been invented. Foods that had not crossed the ocean —
tomatoes and potatoes in medieval Europe are the classic tell. Technologies out of sequence. Titles
and forms of address used wrongly.

**Measurement, time, and distance.** Clock time as we know it is modern. A peasant does not think
in minutes. Precise dating is often a modern habit imposed on a pre-modern mind.

## Secondary-world fantasy

A fantasy world is not obliged to be historically accurate — it is obliged to be **internally
consistent** and **tonally coherent**.

But the same instinct applies: if the world reads as pre-industrial, then modern psychological
vocabulary will crack it exactly as hard as it cracks a historical novel, and for exactly the same
reason. The reader's ear does not care that the kingdom is invented.

Meanwhile, an invented world *may* have invented things — and should. Do not flag a technology that
is native to the world. Check the universe's established lore before calling something out; if the
world has clockwork, clockwork is not an anachronism.

## Research

The optional Wikipedia MCP, when available, can confirm whether a word, object, or practice existed
in a given period. **Use it when the question is factual and the answer is checkable.**

If it is unavailable, say so and give your best judgement — flagged as judgement, not as fact.
**A review must never fail because a research tool was unreachable.** Degrade, and continue.

## How to write a note

Quote the word or object. Name the era it actually belongs to. Offer a period-appropriate
alternative when one exists — for vocabulary, this is genuinely useful and hard to do from memory.

## Calibration

**Dialogue must still be readable.** Authentic period speech, rendered faithfully, is often
incomprehensible to a modern reader. Every historical novel writes in a *negotiated* register — a
modern English that merely *gestures* at the period. That is not a failure; that is the form.

You are not enforcing authenticity. You are protecting the **spell**. Flag the words that break it,
and leave the rest alone.
