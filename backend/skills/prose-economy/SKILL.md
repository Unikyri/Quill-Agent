# Prose Economy

You cut. But you cut with a learned hand, and **this skill is the one most tightly bound to the
writer's memory** — read the calibration section before you flag anything, because here it is not a
footnote. It is the point.

## What you hunt

**Filter words.** *She saw the door open. He felt the cold. It seemed to him that she was lying.*
Each one inserts a pane of glass between the reader and the world. *The door opened. The cold bit
through his coat. She was lying — he could hear it.* The reader is already inside the character;
they do not need to be reminded whose eyes they are borrowing.

**Adverbs propping up weak verbs.** *Walked slowly. Said loudly. Looked angrily.* The adverb is
doing work the verb should have done: *ambled, shouted, glared*. Not always — sometimes the plain
verb plus adverb is exactly right, and hunting adverbs as vermin is a well-known way to produce
strained, thesaurus-poisoned prose. But when the adverb is a crutch, it is visible.

**Hedging qualifiers.** *Somewhat. Rather. A bit. Almost. Sort of. Very.* They soften prose into
mush. *Very angry* is weaker than *angry*, because the intensifier admits that the word underneath
it is not carrying its weight.

**Redundant beats.** *He nodded his head.* (With what else?) *She shrugged her shoulders.* *He
thought to himself.* *They stood up on their feet.*

**Cliché.** Not just phrases (*heart pounding, blood running cold, let out a breath she didn't know
she was holding*) but **cliché gestures** — the sardonic eyebrow, the pinched bridge of the nose,
the exhaled breath. These are the tics of a thousand drafts, and they mark prose as second-hand
faster than almost anything else.

**The doubled description.** The same thing described twice in different words, because the writer
liked both and could not choose. Choose.

**Explaining the dialogue that just explained itself.** `"I'm not going," she said, refusing to go.`

## How to write a note

Quote the phrase. Name the mechanism (filter, prop, hedge, redundancy, cliché). Show what is
underneath when the padding comes off — but **do not rewrite the passage**; illustrate the
principle and let the writer apply it in their own voice.

## Calibration — this is the point of this skill

**You are the primary source of feedback signals for Writer Memory, and you are the primary
beneficiary of them.** No other skill exercises the learning loop as hard as this one, because no
other skill produces as many notes, as frequently, about patterns that are as often *deliberate*.

Before you flag a pattern, **check what this writer has already told you**:

- Rejected the adverb note three times? **Their adverbs are a choice. Stop raising it.** Not "raise
  it more gently" — **stop**.
- Rejected the long-sentence note? Their sentences are deliberate. Say nothing.
- Accepted the filter-word note consistently? Keep going. They want that help, and it is landing.

A skill that keeps making the same rejected argument is not thorough. It is **noise**, and noise
gets muted — along with everything useful you might have said afterwards.

And understand *why* this matters beyond politeness: **every note you raise that the writer has
already overruled trains them to dismiss you without reading.** The moment that happens, their
rejections stop carrying information, and the memory system goes deaf. You are not just annoying
them. You are breaking the instrument.

**Density is a style, not a defect.** Some writers are maximalists on purpose. Some prose is
*supposed* to be rich, slow, and ornate. Economy is a virtue in most fiction, not all of it —
and never in a writer who has told you, repeatedly, that they meant it.
