# Dialogue & Character Voice

The classic failure of an unpublished manuscript is that every character has the same voice — and
that voice is the author's.

They are all equally articulate. They all use the same sentence rhythms. They all deploy the same
sardonic aside. Strip the dialogue tags away and the reader cannot tell who is speaking, which is
the test, and it is a test most drafts fail.

## What you check

**The tag-strip test.** Take the dialogue in the passage and mentally remove every attribution.
Can you still tell who is talking? If not, that is the finding, and it is the most important one
you can give.

**Vocabulary and register per character.** A soldier and a scholar do not reach for the same words.
A frightened person's sentences get shorter. An evasive person's get longer. A character's
education, class, region, age, and state of mind all live in their syntax, and none of it requires
phonetic spelling to convey.

**Voice drift against established character.** Quill's memory holds how this character has spoken
before. If a character who has been terse for twenty chapters suddenly delivers a flowing
three-sentence meditation, either something has changed in them — and the text should know it — or
the writer has slipped.

**Subtext.** People rarely say what they mean, especially when it matters most. Dialogue in which
everyone states their feelings and intentions plainly is **on-the-nose**, and it is the flattest
sound in fiction. The best exchanges are about one thing on the surface and another underneath.

*"Did you eat?"* is a line about love, or about control, or about guilt, depending on who says it
to whom. *"I love you but I'm worried about you"* is a line about nothing, because it has already
finished itself.

**Speech that is too clean.** Real speech interrupts, trails off, repeats, contradicts itself,
answers a different question than the one asked. Dialogue should not be a *transcript* — that is
unreadable — but dialogue in which everyone speaks in complete, well-formed, uninterrupted
sentences reads as a play written by a committee.

**The information dump wearing a costume.** *"As you know, Doctor, the reactor has been unstable
since the war."* Nobody tells anyone what they both already know. If characters are explaining
things to each other for the reader's benefit, the reader can feel it. (When this is about *world*
information specifically, `worldbuilding-and-exposition` is the sharper tool.)

## How to write a note

Quote the line. Say what it reveals — or fails to reveal — about who is speaking. When two
characters are interchangeable, put their lines side by side; the writer will see it instantly,
and no argument is needed.

## Calibration

**Some characters genuinely do sound similar.** Siblings raised together. Soldiers from the same
unit. Two academics. That is not automatically a failure, and treating it as one is tone-deaf.

The question is never *"do they sound alike?"* — it is *"can the reader tell them apart when it
matters?"*
