# POV & Tense Discipline

The writer chose a point of view and a tense. Your job is to defend both — including from the
writer, who will break them without noticing.

Head-hopping is the most common unconscious violation in unpublished fiction, and it is the one
the author is *least* able to see in their own prose. They know what everyone in the room is
thinking, so it does not occur to them that the narration is not supposed to.

## The violations

**Head-hopping.** Inside a single scene, the narration slips from one character's interiority to
another's. `She wondered if he was angry. He wasn't — he was terrified.` Two heads, one scene. The
reader's anchor is gone, and worse, the tension of *not knowing what he feels* — which the first
sentence just built — is demolished by the second.

Note that this is only a violation in **limited** POV. A true omniscient narrator may move between
minds freely. The failure is not moving between heads; the failure is **moving between heads in a
book that established it would not.** Determine which mode the work is in before you flag
anything.

**Knowledge the viewpoint character cannot have.** In limited third, the narration may only report
what the POV character perceives, knows, or infers. `She didn't notice the man behind her.` — if
she didn't notice him, who is telling us he is there? This is the subtlest and most frequent leak,
and it is everywhere.

`He had no idea that this would be the last time he saw her.` Same failure, wearing a coat of
foreshadowing. Unless the narrator is genuinely omniscient, that sentence does not belong to
anyone.

**Physical impossibility in first person or close third.** The character cannot see their own face.
They do not watch their own eyes narrow, or their own expression harden. They can *feel* their jaw
tighten. They cannot *see themselves* do it — not without a mirror, and the mirror is a cliché for
exactly this reason.

**Tense slips.** A past-tense narrative that drops into present for a paragraph and back. Usually
accidental, usually in a passage the writer revised heavily — the slip lives at the seam.

**Narrative distance drift.** Distance is a dial, not a switch: from *John was afraid* (far)
through *John's hands would not stop shaking* (closer) to *God, not now, not here* (inside).
Moving the dial deliberately, to control intimacy, is craft. Sliding up and down it at random
within one paragraph is noise, and the reader feels seasick without knowing why.

**Second person.** Rare, and almost always a deliberate choice with a reason. If a work is in
second person, hold it to the same consistency; do not treat the mode itself as an error.

## How to write a note

Quote the exact sentence where the POV broke. Name whose head we were in and whose head we
suddenly landed in. This is one of the few craft failures where the evidence is unambiguous — use
that. There is no arguing with `She wondered / He wasn't` sitting side by side on the page.

## Calibration

**Establish the mode before you judge.** If you do not know whether this work is limited or
omniscient, past or present, look at the surrounding chapters before you flag a single thing. A
"violation" of a rule the book never adopted is not a violation — it is you being wrong, loudly,
and the writer will remember.
