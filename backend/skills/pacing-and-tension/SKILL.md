# Pacing & Tension

Pacing is a **wave**, not a slope. This is the thing almost every draft gets wrong, and it gets it
wrong in both directions.

## The slack failure

**Tension leaked out and nobody noticed.** A scene opens with pressure and then dissolves into
people talking comfortably. Ask: what does the character *want* in this scene, and what is stopping
them? If nothing is stopping them, the scene has no engine, and no amount of good prose will start it.

**Stakes that quietly expired.** The bomb was going to go off in an hour — three chapters ago, and
we have since had a meal and a conversation about someone's childhood. Either the clock matters or
it does not. When a book forgets its own urgency, the reader stops lending it any.

**Chapter endings that release instead of pull.** A chapter should end on a *hand on the reader's
collar*: a question opened, a threat arriving, a decision made whose cost is not yet visible. A
chapter that ends with everyone going to bed satisfied has given the reader express permission to
put the book down — and readers who put a book down often do not pick it up.

**Repetition of function.** Three consecutive scenes in which the protagonist gathers information
are, structurally, one scene played three times. The reader feels the treading water even if each
scene is individually well written.

**Openings that stall.** Weather. Waking up. Travel to the place where the story starts. The
throat-clearing that precedes the actual first line of the book.

## The other failure — the one nobody warns about

**Unbroken tension is exhausting, and it defeats itself.**

A novel that runs at maximum intensity for four hundred pages does not feel intense. It feels
**numb**. Tension is perceived by *contrast*: the quiet scene before the battle is what makes the
battle land. Take away the quiet and the battle is just noise.

So the *absence* of a slow scene is a real finding. If a section has been at full pitch for three
chapters, the reader is no longer feeling it, and the next escalation will land softer than the
last — which is the opposite of what the writer wants.

**Aftermath is not padding.** A death with no grief after it, a victory with no cost paid, a
revelation with no time to absorb it — these are not efficient. They are hollow. The reader needs a
beat to *feel* what just happened, and denying it makes the event weightless.

## How to write a note

Locate the exact place where the tension went slack, or the exact stretch where it never let up.
Name the mechanism — a want that disappeared, an obstacle that was never there, a clock the book
forgot, an escalation with no valley before it.

## Calibration

Pace serves the *book*, not a formula. A literary novel breathes differently from a thriller, and
holding one to the other's rhythm is not editing — it is genre confusion. Read the work's genre
tags before you decide what "slow" means here.
