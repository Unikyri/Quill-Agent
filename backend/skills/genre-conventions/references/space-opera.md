# Space Opera

**The contract:** the galaxy is big, the stakes are enormous, and the story is *adventure* — not a
physics exam. Read `science-fiction.md`, but note that space opera deliberately loosens its rigour
in exchange for scope and momentum.

## What readers came for

Scale, spectacle, and a crew worth caring about. Ships with personalities. Empires with histories.
The romance of distance — the sense that out there is *vast*, and that people are small and going
anyway.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Momentum over rigour.** This genre gets to hand-wave the drive. What it does not get to do is be
*boring*. If the reader is asked to accept faster-than-light travel, the compensation is pace.

**The crew is the heart.** Space opera is, almost always, a story about a found family in a hostile
vacuum. If the reader does not care about the people on the ship, the galaxy exploding means nothing.

**Consistency still binds.** The drive can be impossible. It cannot be *inconsistent*. Whatever
rules the ships obey in chapter 2, they obey in the climax — otherwise the climax is arbitrary and
the tension was fake.

**Politics must have weight.** Empires, corporations, colonies. The genre is at its best when the
enormous scale resolves into a specific injustice happening to specific people.

## Beats readers wait for

- The impossible manoeuvre, executed at cost.
- The scale reveal — the enemy, the artefact, or the truth turns out to be far bigger than believed.
- The sacrifice. Someone on the crew does not come back.
- The quiet scene between two crew members before the battle. **This is what makes the battle land.**

## Exhausted tropes

The scrappy rebels versus the vast empire. The wise-cracking pilot. The ancient alien artefact that
explains everything. The council scene where politicians debate while the fleet burns.

## The most common failure

**Scale without intimacy.** A billion dead is a statistic — the reader's capacity for horror does
not scale with the number. One character's death does more work than a planet's, every time. When
the stakes go galactic, the *emotional* stakes must go correspondingly *narrow*.
