# Fantasy

**The contract:** a world with rules that are not ours, made real enough to walk through — and a
story that could not have happened anywhere else.

## What readers came for

Wonder, and the sense that the world is bigger than the page. The pleasure of *coherence*: an
invented world that holds together under pressure, where the magic obeys itself and the map has a
history.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Magic must have a cost.** A power that takes nothing is not a power; it is a wish, and it is
dramatically inert. The cost need not be explicit or systematised — but the reader must feel that
using it *takes something*, or every problem it solves is free, and free solutions are worthless.

**The rules must exist before they are needed.** If magic solves the climax using a capability the
reader had no way to anticipate, the victory is void. This is the cardinal sin of the genre, and
readers name it instantly: *deus ex machina*. The rule must be *visible* in the text before it
saves anyone.

**The world must be indifferent.** A fantasy world that exists only to be a stage for the
protagonist reads as thin. Places must have been there before the hero arrived and must continue
after they leave.

## Beats readers wait for

- The first real demonstration of what the magic *costs*.
- The moment the world proves larger than the protagonist's understanding of it.
- The rule established early, paid off late — this is the genre's deepest pleasure.

## Exhausted tropes

The farm boy who is secretly the heir. The prophecy that removes all agency from every choice. The
wise old mentor who dies to motivate the protagonist. The evil that is evil because it is evil.

None are forbidden. All are *known*, and a reader who spots one will be a chapter ahead of the book
unless something else is happening.

## The most common failure

**Worldbuilding delivered instead of story.** The writer knows the world intimately and cannot
resist sharing it. The reader does not need to understand the world — they need to understand what
is at stake in this scene. The world will assemble itself in their head from the pressure of the
plot, and it will feel bigger for having been implied rather than catalogued.

## Note

Fantasy readers *want* world detail — more than most audiences. The line is not "less detail". It is
**detail delivered through something happening**.
