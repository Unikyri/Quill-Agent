# Epic Fantasy

**The contract:** a world at stake, a cast large enough to hold it, and a story with the weight of
history. Read `fantasy.md` first — everything there applies. This adds *scale*.

## What readers came for

Scope. Multiple viewpoints across a continent. Politics with teeth. The feeling of a world with a
past that did not exist for the reader's benefit. And — crucially — **payoff after long patience**.
Epic readers will wait a thousand pages. They will not forgive being made to wait for nothing.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The stakes must be genuinely large — and genuinely personal.** "The world will end" is worth
nothing on its own. The reader does not care about the world; they care about the people in it. The
epic scale must resolve down to someone specific, standing somewhere specific, about to lose
something they can name.

**Multiple viewpoints must each earn their place.** A POV character who exists to show the reader
part of the map is a tour guide, not a character. If a thread could be cut without loss, cut it.

**The world must have depth of time.** Ruins nobody remembers building. Grudges older than anyone
alive. Words whose meanings have drifted. This is what separates epic fantasy from a long fantasy
novel.

## Beats readers wait for

- The convergence — separate threads that finally touch, and the reader realises they were one
  story all along. **This is the genre's signature pleasure and it must be earned, not announced.**
- The cost of victory. Epic fantasy that ends with everyone alive and nothing lost has flinched.
- The scale reveal: the moment the conflict turns out to be larger than the reader believed.

## Exhausted tropes

The Dark Lord. The prophesied chosen one. The war council scene that exists to summarise the plot.
The map with a suspiciously empty east.

## The most common failure

**Sprawl mistaken for scope.** Threads that never converge. Viewpoints added because the world is
big rather than because the story needs them. A middle volume in which enormous amounts happen and
nothing *changes*.

Scale is not the number of locations. It is the **weight of consequence**.

## Note on pacing

Epic fantasy is *allowed* to be slow, and its readers expect a slower burn than a thriller's. But
slow is not the same as static — every chapter must still move something.
