# Historical

**The contract:** the past, made inhabitable — accurate enough to trust, alive enough to feel.

Pair this with the `period-register` skill, which handles anachronism at the sentence level. This
file covers the *narrative* obligations.

## What readers came for

**Immersion in a world that actually existed.** The pleasure is double: the story, and the sense of
having *been* somewhere real. Historical readers are often serious amateur historians and they read
partly to catch you.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Accuracy where it is checkable.** Dates, technologies, foods, clothing, titles, currencies,
distances, how long it took to get anywhere. Errors here are found instantly and reviewed publicly.

**Period mentality, not just period furniture.** This is the deeper and far more commonly failed
obligation. People of other eras did not think as we think. Their assumptions about God, death,
class, childhood, the body, and the self were *structurally* different — not merely less enlightened.
A historical novel populated by modern minds in period clothing is costume drama, and it is the
genre's most common failure.

**Modern protagonists are permitted — but must cost.** A character who holds views ahead of their
time is legitimate; history is full of them. But they must hold those views **in the terms available
to them**, and they must meet friction. A protagonist whose progressive positions are shared by
everyone sympathetic and opposed only by villains has flattened the past into a morality play.

**Research must be invisible.** The writer read forty books. The reader must not have to.

**Real historical figures carry obligations.** They may be interpreted; they should not be
*traduced* without cause.

## Beats readers wait for

- The texture of daily life, delivered through use rather than description.
- The historical event, experienced from below — by someone who does not know how it turns out.
- The collision between the protagonist's needs and the period's constraints. **This is the genre's
  engine.**

## Exhausted tropes

The feisty heroine who defies every convention with no consequences. The unusually hygienic
peasantry. The character who happens to witness every major event of the era.

## The most common failure

**The research showing.** Three paragraphs on the construction of a longbow because the writer
learned it and could not bear to waste it. The reader wants the *world*, not the bibliography.

**The second most common failure:** the past as a place where everyone was stupid and cruel, except
the protagonist. It is condescension, and it is dull.
