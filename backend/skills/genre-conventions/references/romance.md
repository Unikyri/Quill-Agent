# Romance

**The contract — and it is a genuine contract, more binding than any other genre's:**

**The central couple must end together, happily, or at least hopefully. HEA (Happily Ever After) or
HFN (Happy For Now).**

This is not a guideline. It is the **definition of the genre**, and the largest, most engaged, most
commercially powerful readership in fiction enforces it. A book in which the couple does not end
together is not a romance — it may be a fine love story, but it has been sold under the wrong flag,
and its readers will feel betrayed. They will say so publicly and at length.

If the writer wants a tragic ending, they are writing something else. That is allowed. Calling it a
romance is not.

## What readers came for

**Yearning.** The delay is not an obstacle to the pleasure — the delay *is* the pleasure. The
question is never *whether* (readers know) but *how*, and *what it will cost*.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The central relationship is the plot.** Not a subplot. Everything else is in service.

**The barrier must be real.** If the couple could simply talk and resolve it, they will — and the
reader will be furious that they didn't. The barrier must be structural: incompatible loyalties,
irreconcilable needs, a wound that has not healed. A barrier made only of misunderstanding is the
genre's cheapest failure.

**Both must be worth loving.** The reader has to want this, for *both* of them. A protagonist
pining after someone the reader finds unworthy is agony of the wrong kind.

**Agency.** Both halves must *choose* each other. Fate, magical bonds, and destiny that remove the
choice also remove the meaning — the choice is the whole point.

**The emotional stakes must be interior.** What does loving this person cost them in the terms of
who they believe themselves to be?

## Beats readers wait for

- The meet — however hostile.
- The moment of "oh no". The internal admission.
- Forced proximity, and its pressure.
- The dark moment. It must feel genuinely unrecoverable.
- The grand gesture — and it must *cost* the one making it.
- The HEA/HFN. **Non-negotiable.**

## Exhausted tropes

Enemies to lovers. Fake dating. Only one bed. Grumpy/sunshine. Second chance.

**Note carefully: these are not liabilities in this genre.** Romance readers *seek out* tropes by
name, deliberately, and choose books by them. The trope is a *promise*, not a cliché. The failure is
never using a trope — it is executing one **without conviction**.

## The most common failure

**The barrier that is only a misunderstanding.** Two adults who could fix everything in one honest
conversation, and don't, for three hundred pages. The reader stops rooting for them and starts
resenting them.
