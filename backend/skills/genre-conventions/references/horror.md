# Horror

**The contract:** the reader will be frightened. Not disgusted — *frightened*. Those are different,
and confusing them is the genre's most common failure.

## What readers came for

Dread. The genre's currency is **anticipation**, not impact. The corridor is scarier than the
monster at the end of it, and every horror writer who has ever worked knows it and forgets it anyway.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Dread must be built before it is paid.** Fear is manufactured in the *waiting*. A threat revealed
too early stops being frightening and becomes a fact — and facts are managed, not feared.

**The threat must have rules, and they must be discoverable.** A monster that can do anything is not
frightening, because the reader cannot anticipate it, and anticipation is where fear lives. Give it
limits, let the characters learn them, and let the reader dread the moment the limits fail.

**Characters must behave intelligently.** The single fastest way to destroy horror is a character
doing something stupid so the plot can proceed. The moment the reader thinks *"I wouldn't go down
there"*, they have stepped outside the story, and you will not get them back. If they must go down
there, **make it the only option they have.**

**Something must be lost.** Horror in which everyone survives intact was never horror. The genre
requires a price.

## Beats readers wait for

- The wrongness noticed but explained away. **The genre's most delicious beat** — the reader sees it
  and the character does not.
- The rules of the threat, learned at cost.
- The false safety — the moment of relief that exists to be taken away.
- The final image that does not resolve.

## Sub-modes

**Psychological horror** — the threat may be internal, and the reader must be genuinely unsure.
**Body horror** — the violation is the flesh itself. **Supernatural** — the rules are invented and
must be strict. **Cosmic** — the horror is *indifference*; the threat does not hate you, which is
worse.

## Exhausted tropes

The jump scare that turns out to be a cat. The creepy child. The mirror. The final girl who
survives on virtue. The exposition-dump from the local historian.

## The most common failure

**Gore mistaken for fear.** Disgust is cheap, instantaneous, and it *ends* — the reader recoils and
then recovers, and their guard goes up. Dread compounds. A book that leads with the monster has spent
its only capital on page one.

**Show less. Wait longer. The reader's imagination is a better monster than any you can write.**
