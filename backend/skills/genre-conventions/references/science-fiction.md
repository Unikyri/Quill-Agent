# Science Fiction

**The contract:** change one thing about how the world works, and follow the consequences honestly.

## What readers came for

The rigorous *what if*. Not gadgets — **consequences**. The pleasure of watching a premise unfold
into implications the reader had not thought of, and recognising each one as inevitable in
hindsight.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The premise must be followed through.** If your technology exists, it changes *everything* it
touches: the economy, the law, the family, the war, the way people speak about themselves. A world
with faster-than-light travel and 21st-century politics is a world that has not been thought about.
This is the failure SF readers punish hardest, and they punish it without mercy.

**Internal consistency over real-world accuracy.** The science may be invented. It may not be
*inconsistent*. A rule established in chapter 3 must still hold in chapter 30, and a reader who
notices otherwise is gone.

**The idea must cost someone something.** A premise explored in the abstract is an essay. Science
fiction requires a person standing inside the consequence, paying for it.

## Hardness

Hard SF holds itself to known physics and is judged by that standard. Soft SF is free with its
science and is judged on its ideas and its people. **Both are legitimate. What is not legitimate is
being unclear which one you are** — a book that signals rigour and then hand-waves has broken faith,
and one that signals adventure and then lectures has bored everyone.

## Beats readers wait for

- The implication nobody saw coming — the second-order effect that reframes the premise.
- The moment the technology's cost lands on a person the reader cares about.
- The reveal that the *human* problem was never solved by the technology, only relocated.

## Exhausted tropes

The AI that becomes conscious and turns on us. The dystopia justified in a single monologue by a
villain who explains the whole system. The scientist who ignored the warnings.

## The most common failure

**The lecture.** The writer researched, and the research is *right there*, in a character's mouth,
for three pages. The reader wants the *consequence* of the idea, not its documentation. Deliver
science through what it *does* to people.

## Note

SF readers *do* want technical texture — more than most audiences. The line is not "explain less".
It is **explain in motion**.
