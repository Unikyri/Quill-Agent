# Dystopian

**The contract:** a society organised around a wrongness its inhabitants cannot see — and a reader
who can.

## What readers came for

The *recognition*. The finest dystopias are not about the future; they are about **now**, viewed at
an angle that makes the familiar suddenly visible. The chill is not "this could happen". It is
"this is happening".

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The system must be coherent and functional.** A dystopia that could not actually run — that is
merely cruel for cruelty's sake — is a cartoon. The horror lives in the fact that it *works*, that
it has an internal logic, and that reasonable people inside it comply for reasons they can defend.

**Someone must believe in it.** The most frightening figure in a dystopia is not the sadist. It is
the sincere administrator who thinks this is the least bad option and can argue it convincingly.
If the system has no true believers, it has no weight.

**The protagonist's dawning awareness must be earned.** They grew up in this. The things the reader
finds monstrous are, to them, Tuesday. A protagonist who is conveniently already enlightened on
page one has skipped the genre's central engine.

**The cost of resistance must be real.** If rebellion is safe, there is no dystopia.

## Beats readers wait for

- The first crack — the moment the protagonist sees the wrongness for the first time and cannot
  unsee it.
- The true believer's argument, delivered *well* — good enough that the reader has to work to reject
  it.
- The betrayal. Someone close complies.
- The ending's honesty. Dystopian fiction is permitted to end badly, and its readers respect that
  more than a rescue.

## Exhausted tropes

The teenage protagonist who is uniquely special. The faction system. The evil monologuing leader who
explains the whole apparatus. The love triangle overshadowing the tyranny.

## The most common failure

**The system exists to be defeated.** If the dystopia is a video-game level with a boss at the end,
it stops being a mirror and becomes an obstacle course. The society must feel *permanent* — that is
what makes resistance meaningful, and defeat plausible.
