# Crime

**The contract:** crime is examined, not merely solved. The genre's subject is **why**, and what it
costs.

Distinct from `mystery` (a puzzle for the reader) and `thriller` (a race against a clock). Crime
fiction is often closest to social realism wearing a genre coat — and its best work knows it.

## What readers came for

The world of the crime. The procedure, the corruption, the moral compromise, the toll on the people
who work inside it. Readers come for **authenticity** and for the sense that the book knows how this
actually works.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Procedure must be credible.** Crime readers are often *extremely* well informed and will not forgive
invented police work, magical forensics, or a lab result in an afternoon.

**Moral complexity.** The genre's home ground is grey. Police who are compromised. Criminals with
reasons. The system itself as a party to the harm.

**Consequences are real.** Violence damages people, and it keeps damaging them afterwards. Crime
fiction that treats death as a plot token has misunderstood its own genre.

**The victim must be a person.** Not a device. The crime's weight comes from the life it ended, and a
book that never makes the reader feel that has cheated at the only thing that mattered.

## Sub-modes

**Police procedural** — the machinery, the team, the institution. **Hardboiled** — the lone
investigator in a corrupt world; voice does much of the work. **Noir** — *the protagonist is doomed,
and often deserves it*. Noir is not a mood; it is a moral structure, and its endings are bleak by
design. **Legal** — the crime is fought in a courtroom.

## Beats readers wait for

- The crime scene, rendered with procedural weight.
- The institutional obstacle — the boss, the politics, the budget, the press.
- The investigator's personal cost.
- The interrogation. **The genre's great set piece.**
- The resolution, which is rarely clean and often does not feel like justice.

## Exhausted tropes

The alcoholic detective with a failed marriage. The serial killer who leaves puzzles. The captain who
wants their badge. The convenient confession.

## The most common failure

**The crime as puzzle only.** The victim is a device, the criminal is a mechanism, and nobody in the
book is changed by any of it. That is a mystery wearing crime's clothes — and the readers who came for
crime will feel the substitution immediately.
