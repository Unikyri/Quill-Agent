# Gothic

**The contract:** the past is not dead, the house is not innocent, and the atmosphere is the plot.

## What readers came for

**Mood above all.** Gothic is the one genre where atmosphere is not decoration — it is the *engine*.
The decaying house, the weather, the sense of a place that remembers. Readers come for the feeling of
being slowly enclosed.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The setting is a character.** The house, the moor, the crumbling estate. It must have a will, a
history, and an effect on everyone inside it. A gothic novel that could be relocated to a different
building is not gothic.

**The past intrudes on the present.** Secrets, inheritances, buried crimes, a family that did
something and never spoke of it. The story's engine is always **something that was hidden**.

**Confinement.** Physical, social, psychological — usually all three. The protagonist cannot simply
leave, and the reasons they cannot leave are the story.

**Ambiguity is permitted, and prized.** Was it a ghost or a mind coming apart? Gothic is the genre
most comfortable refusing to say, and its readers *like* the refusal.

**Transgression, sensed but not named.** Something in this house is wrong, and it is usually about
power, sex, madness, or blood — the things the family will not discuss.

## Beats readers wait for

- The arrival. The outsider crosses the threshold. **The genre's opening move.**
- The locked room, the forbidden wing, the door that is not to be opened.
- The household's silence — everyone knows something and no one will say it.
- The revelation of the buried crime.
- The house's fate, which is always the family's fate.

## Exhausted tropes

The madwoman in the attic. The brooding master with a secret. The naive governess. The storm that
arrives exactly when the plot requires it.

These are the genre's founding images. Readers will accept them **if the book knows they are
inheritance** — gothic is a genre in permanent conversation with its own history, and a knowing
deployment reads as fluency, not theft.

## The most common failure

**Atmosphere without pressure.** Beautiful, mounting dread — and nothing happening. Gothic is slow,
but slow is not *static*. Something must be closing in, and each chapter must tighten it.

## Note on prose

Gothic tolerates — and often *demands* — richer, denser, more ornate prose than any other genre in
this catalogue. **Do not apply `prose-economy` here with a straight face.** Ornament is the form.
