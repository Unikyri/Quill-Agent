# Adventure

**The contract:** motion, obstacle, and the pleasure of competence under pressure.

## What readers came for

Forward momentum through a world worth crossing. The physical, tactile joy of a plan going wrong and
someone dealing with it. Adventure is the oldest shape in fiction and it still works because it is
*honest* about what it is.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The journey has stakes at every stage.** Not one goal at the end — a *sequence* of obstacles, each
with its own cost, each changing the situation.

**Competence, tested.** The reader wants to watch someone who is *good at this* meet something that
is nearly too much. Not a bumbler (that is comedy), not an invincible (that is boredom).

**The world is dangerous and indifferent.** The mountain does not care. The sea does not care. That
indifference is a large part of the pleasure.

**Physicality.** Adventure lives in the body: cold, hunger, exhaustion, injury, the weight of the
pack. A journey the reader does not feel in their legs has not happened.

**Something must be lost along the way.** A companion, a resource, an illusion. A journey with no
attrition has no weight.

## Beats readers wait for

- The call — and the moment the protagonist commits past the point of return.
- The plan, and the plan's collapse.
- The moment the environment nearly wins.
- The companion who does not make it.
- The arrival, which is never what was expected.

## Exhausted tropes

The map with one convenient route. The wise local guide. The betrayal by the untrustworthy member of
the party (foreshadowed to the point of certainty). The treasure that was the friends we made.

## The most common failure

**Obstacles with no consequence.** The party faces a chasm, crosses it, and is exactly as they were
before. Every obstacle must **take something** — a resource, a member, time, hope — or it was
scenery, and the reader learns to stop worrying.

## Note

Frequently braided with `fantasy`, `science-fiction`, or `historical`. Adventure is a *shape* more
than a setting, and it composes readily. When it does, read the other file too.
