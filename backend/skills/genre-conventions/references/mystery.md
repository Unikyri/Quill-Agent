# Mystery

**The contract:** the reader is playing. **Fair play is absolute.**

Every clue needed to solve the mystery must be *available to the reader* before the solution is
revealed. They may be disguised, buried under misdirection, or hidden in plain sight — but they must
be **there**. A solution that depends on information the reader never had is not clever. It is a
cheat, and mystery readers are the most unforgiving audience in fiction on exactly this point.

## What readers came for

The **puzzle**, and the pleasure of being outwitted *fairly*. The ideal reaction to the reveal is:
*"I should have seen it."* Not *"how could I have known?"* — that is the sound of a broken book.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**Fair play.** Every clue on the page, before the reveal.

**The detective must *detect*.** Solutions arriving by confession, coincidence, or luck steal the
reader's game from them.

**Red herrings must be honest.** Misdirection through *interpretation* is the craft. Misdirection
through *lying to the reader* in the narration is not.

**The culprit must be present and viable.** They must appear early enough that the reader could have
suspected them. A murderer introduced in chapter 28 is a fraud.

**The solution must be the *only* one that fits.** If three suspects could equally have done it, the
puzzle had no answer.

## Beats readers wait for

- The body, or the crime — early. The genre's engine does not start without it.
- The suspects, each with means, motive, and opportunity.
- The clue the reader *saw* but *misread*. **The genre's supreme pleasure.**
- The false solution that collapses.
- The reveal, and the reconstruction — walking the reader back through the clues they had all along.

## Exhausted tropes

The butler. The identical twin. The secret passage. The solution that hinges on a poison nobody had
heard of. The detective who withholds their reasoning purely to preserve the surprise.

That last one is worth dwelling on: a detective who *conceals* their thinking from the reader for no
reason other than the author's convenience is the genre's most irritating cheat.

## The most common failure

**Withholding rather than misdirecting.** The writer, afraid the reader will guess, simply does not
give them the clue. That is not a mystery — it is a magic trick performed in another room.

**Trust the reader to be clever, and be cleverer.**
