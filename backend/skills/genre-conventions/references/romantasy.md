# Romantasy

**The contract:** a fantasy world *and* a central romance, and **both must satisfy**. Read
`romance.md` and `fantasy.md` — this genre inherits the hard obligations of both, and readers
enforce them.

Currently one of the strongest-selling categories in publishing. Its readers are well-read,
demanding, and extremely vocal.

## What readers came for

The magic **and** the yearning, braided. Not a fantasy novel with a subplot. Not a romance with
dragons for decoration. The relationship must be *entangled* with the world's stakes — the magic
should complicate the love, and the love should complicate the magic.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The romance is a main plot, not a subplot.** If the love story could be removed and the book
would still stand, this is not romantasy — it is fantasy with a love interest, and the audience
will feel short-changed.

**The romance obligations hold in full.** An emotionally satisfying ending for the couple is not
optional. Readers of this genre will not accept a tragic ending, and framing it as literary daring
will not save it. (See `romance.md`.)

**The fantasy obligations hold in full.** The magic still needs a cost and rules established before
they are needed. A world that exists only to throw the lovers together is a stage set, and it shows.

**The two must interfere with each other.** This is the actual test of the genre. If the magical
plot and the romantic plot run on parallel tracks and only meet at the end, the book has not fused
anything — it has stapled two books together.

## Beats readers wait for

- The forced proximity, and the antagonism that is obviously something else.
- The moment where the magic's cost is paid *in the relationship*.
- The choice between the world and the person. **This is the genre's defining beat.** Both answers
  are legitimate; ducking the choice is not.
- Yearning sustained. The delay *is* the pleasure — resolve it too early and the engine stops.

## Exhausted tropes

The brooding fae prince. The bond/mate mark that removes all choice from the attraction. The
academy. The enemy who was secretly protecting her the whole time.

Readers of this genre have seen every one of these hundreds of times. They will still enjoy them —
**if the execution knows it is executing a known beat and does something with it.**

## The most common failure

**Unbalanced halves.** A gorgeous world with a perfunctory romance, or a burning romance in a
cardboard world. Whichever half the writer loves less is where the book breaks, and the reader will
find it immediately.
