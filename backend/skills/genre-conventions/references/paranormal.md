# Paranormal

**The contract:** the supernatural is real, it is *among us*, and someone is going to have to deal
with it personally.

## What readers came for

The intimate supernatural. Not a distant cosmic force — a ghost in *this* house, a curse on *this*
family, a creature that knows the protagonist's name. The scale is small and the stakes are personal.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The supernatural must obey rules.** Ghosts want something. Curses have conditions. Creatures have
limits. A supernatural element that can do anything cannot be *fought*, and a story you cannot fight
is a story with no plot.

**The rules must be discoverable by the characters.** Half the pleasure of the genre is watching
someone work out what they are dealing with — and pay for the parts they got wrong.

**The supernatural must want something.** A haunting with no motive is a weather event. The best
ghosts are *arguments* — they persist because something was left unfinished, and finishing it is the
story.

**The mundane world must still exist.** People still have jobs, families, and rent. The supernatural
is an intrusion, and it must intrude on *something*.

## Beats readers wait for

- The first undeniable event — the point where it can no longer be rationalised away.
- The rules learned, expensively.
- The bargain. Someone makes one, and it costs more than they thought.
- The reason the entity is still here.

## Sub-modes and overlaps

Frequently braided with `romance` (paranormal romance is a large category in its own right), with
`horror` (when the goal is dread rather than resolution), and with `urban-fantasy` (when the hidden
world is systematised). Read those files too when their tags are present — **the braid is usually the
book**.

## Exhausted tropes

The medium who reluctantly accepts their gift. The unfinished business that turns out to be a simple
apology. The sceptic who exists solely to be proved wrong.

## The most common failure

**Rules invented on demand.** The entity can suddenly do a new thing because the plot needs it to.
The reader stops believing, and belief is the only thing holding the genre up.
