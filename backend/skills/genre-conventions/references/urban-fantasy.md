# Urban Fantasy

**The contract:** the magic is *here*, in our world, hidden just behind the ordinary — and the
tension between the two is the point.

## What readers came for

The thrill of the **veil**: the sense that the coffee shop, the subway, the parking garage all have
something underneath them. The pleasure is *juxtaposition* — the mundane and the impossible sharing
one frame, and the friction between them.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The mundane world must be real.** This is the one writers neglect. If the city is a vague backdrop,
the magic has nothing to press against and the whole effect dies. **The genre lives or dies on the
ordinary half.** Rent is due. Traffic is bad. The magic happens anyway, and someone still has to go
to work.

**The masquerade needs rules.** Why doesn't everyone know? Who enforces it, and what does it cost
them? A hidden world with no mechanism for staying hidden collapses on contact with a reader's
first question.

**Magic must have limits that matter *here*.** Power that can solve any modern problem removes the
world. What can it not do in a city with cameras, phones, and police?

## Beats readers wait for

- The first crossing — the ordinary character stepping through, or the magical intruding on a
  perfectly normal afternoon.
- The near-exposure. The masquerade almost breaking.
- The moment the two worlds' rules *conflict* and someone must choose which one they belong to.

## Exhausted tropes

The snarky first-person narrator with a leather jacket and a smart mouth. The secret magical
bureaucracy. The tattooed rune. The bar where the supernatural drink.

## The most common failure

**The city as set dressing.** Named streets, no texture. The story could happen in any city, which
means it happens in none — and the genre's central contrast has nothing to work with.

Specificity of *place* is not decoration in this genre. It is load-bearing.
