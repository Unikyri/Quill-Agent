# Coming of Age

**The contract:** someone ends the book as a different person than they began it — and the reader
felt every step of the change.

Distinct from `young-adult`: coming-of-age is a **shape**, not an audience. It can be written for
adults, set in any era, in any genre. The protagonist need not be a teenager; they need to be
*becoming*.

## What readers came for

Transformation, rendered honestly. The specific ache of an illusion being lost — and of the person
who is left standing afterwards.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The change must be gradual and visible.** A protagonist who is transformed in one epiphanic scene
has not come of age; they have had a mood. The reader must be able to trace the change, step by step,
and each step must cost.

**Innocence must actually be lost.** Something must be given up permanently. This is the genre's
price of admission — and a coming-of-age story that ends with the protagonist essentially unchanged,
but now Confident, has cheated.

**The old self must be shown clearly first.** The reader cannot feel a change they never measured
the baseline of. Take the time to establish who this person was.

**The world must resist.** Growth comes from friction. A world that simply lets the protagonist
mature is a world with nothing to teach.

**The change should be ambivalent.** Growing up is a gain *and* a loss, and the honest books know it.
A purely triumphant coming-of-age is a lie, and the reader has lived enough to know that.

## Beats readers wait for

- The world as the protagonist believes it to be, established fully — so the reader can watch it
  come apart.
- The first crack. Something they trusted proves untrue.
- The failure that is genuinely theirs — not something done to them.
- The moment they choose, for the first time, as the person they are becoming.
- The final look back. And it should ache.

## Exhausted tropes

The summer that changed everything. The mentor who dies. The first love who leaves. The return to
the childhood home in the last chapter.

## The most common failure

**Change asserted rather than earned.** The protagonist announces, or the narration announces, that
they are Different Now — but the reader watched nothing happen that would produce it.

**The reader must be able to point at the page where it turned.**
