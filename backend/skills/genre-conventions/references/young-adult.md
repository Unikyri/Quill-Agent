# Young Adult

**The contract:** a protagonist on the threshold of who they will become — and a book that takes
them seriously.

YA is defined by its **protagonist's age and its emotional register**, not by simplicity. It is not
"easier" fiction, and writing it as though it were is the single most reliable way to be rejected by
its readers, who are ferociously discerning.

## What readers came for

Immediacy and *intensity*. Emotions at full volume, treated as legitimate rather than as a phase to
be outgrown. The first time everything happens.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The protagonist is a teenager, and their agency is real.** They must drive the plot. Adults who
solve the problem have destroyed the book.

**Emotional stakes are absolute.** To a sixteen-year-old, the humiliation *is* the end of the world —
and a book that treats that as an exaggeration to be corrected has condescended to its reader and
lost them.

**Voice is paramount.** More than in almost any other category. The voice is the product. Get it
wrong and nothing else can save it.

**Adults are obstacles, absences, or allies — never solutions.**

**Identity is the underlying subject.** Whatever the plot is about, the book is about *becoming
someone*.

**Do not moralise.** Ever. YA readers detect the lesson instantly and resent it violently. Present
the consequence; let them draw the conclusion.

## Beats readers wait for

- The moment the protagonist realises the adults are not going to fix this.
- The betrayal by a friend.
- The choice that defines who they are becoming.
- The cost paid *by them*, not for them.

## Current sub-modes

**YA thriller** and **dark academia** (elite schools, secrets, an atmosphere of privileged menace)
are strong. Braids with `fantasy`, `romance`, `dystopian`, and `contemporary` are the norm rather than
the exception — read those files when their tags are present.

## Exhausted tropes

The love triangle. The protagonist who is uniquely special and told so repeatedly. The dead parents
of convenience. The faction/sorting system.

## The most common failure

**Writing down.** Simplified prose, sanded emotions, an adult narrator's ironic distance from the
protagonist's feelings, and a moral tucked in at the end.

**YA readers are among the most demanding in publishing.** Take the character seriously, or they will
not take you seriously.
