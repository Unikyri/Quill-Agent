# Literary

**The contract:** the sentence matters, the interior matters, and the reader is trusted.

Literary fiction is not a genre of *plot*; it is a genre of **attention**. Its conventions are real,
but they are conventions of emphasis rather than of event — and applying commercial-genre logic to
it is a category error.

## What readers came for

Prose worth slowing down for. Characters rendered with enough interiority that they feel like
people rather than functions. Ambiguity that is not a failure to decide but a refusal to simplify.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**The prose carries weight of its own.** In a thriller, prose is a window; the reader should not
notice it. In literary fiction, the prose is *part of the meaning*, and a merely serviceable sentence
is a missed opportunity.

**Interiority over incident.** What happens matters less than what it *means* to the person it
happens to. A literary novel may have very little plot and still be taut, if the interior pressure
is high.

**The reader is trusted.** Do not explain the theme. Do not close the meaning. Literary readers
resent being told what to think about what they have just read — the inference *is* the pleasure.

**Ambiguity is permitted, and often required.** Endings may not resolve. Questions may not be
answered. This is not evasion; it is the form.

**Character over sympathy.** Protagonists need not be likeable. They must be *interesting*, and they
must be rendered with enough honesty that the reader understands them from inside.

## Beats readers wait for

There is no beat sheet, and imposing one is the fastest way to write a bad literary novel. But there
is almost always:

- A pressure that does not announce itself.
- A moment of recognition — the character sees something about themselves, or fails to, in a way the
  reader does not.
- An ending that reframes rather than resolves.

## Exhausted tropes

The unhappy academic and their affair. The dying parent as a structuring device. The novel about a
novelist. Ambiguity used as a substitute for having decided anything.

That last one is the genre's characteristic dishonesty, and it is worth naming: **vagueness is not
depth.** An ending that is unresolved because the writer chose it reads differently from one that is
unresolved because the writer did not know.

## The most common failure

**Beautiful sentences in service of nothing.** Prose that is admired and not *felt*. Literary
fiction still needs pressure, want, and consequence — it merely locates them inside the person rather
than outside.

## Note on pacing and economy

**Do not apply thriller pacing here, and do not apply `prose-economy` with a heavy hand.** Slowness
is a legitimate choice, and density is often the point. Judge this work by its own contract.
