# Thriller

**The contract:** the reader will not be able to stop. **Momentum is the product.**

## What readers came for

Propulsion. The physical inability to put the book down. Where a mystery asks *"who did it?"*, a
thriller asks **"will they make it?"** — and the difference reorganises everything.

## Non-negotiable conventions

**A clock.** Something is coming, and there is not enough time. The clock is the genre's engine and
it must be *visible*. Without one, tension leaks out no matter how good the prose is.

**The threat must be active.** A thriller villain is not waiting to be discovered — they are *doing
something*, right now, and getting closer.

**The protagonist must be outmatched.** If they can handle this comfortably, there is no thriller.
Competence is fine; *sufficient* competence is fatal.

**Escalation.** Every solution creates a worse problem. The protagonist's cleverness must keep
*raising* the stakes, not lowering them.

**Chapter endings must pull.** This genre lives on the compulsion to read one more chapter. A chapter
that ends on a resolved beat has given the reader permission to stop, and some of them will.

**The stakes must be personal.** "Millions will die" is abstract. "Millions will die, and one of them
is his daughter" is a thriller.

## Beats readers wait for

- The inciting violation — the ordinary world broken, fast. **Thrillers start late and move.**
- The moment the protagonist realises how far this goes.
- The betrayal by the trusted ally.
- The false victory, immediately overturned.
- The confrontation, in which the protagonist must pay something to win.

## Sub-modes

**Techno-thriller** (currently strong: AI, surveillance, privacy). **Domestic suspense** (the threat
is inside the marriage, the family, the house — one of the most commercially powerful current forms).
**Legal, political, espionage** — same engine, different furniture.

## Exhausted tropes

The rogue agent framed for a murder. The villain who explains the plan instead of executing it. The
protagonist who withholds information from allies for no reason. The mole.

## The most common failure

**Pause for backstory.** The chase stops so the reader can learn about the protagonist's childhood.
The engine dies, and restarting it costs more than the backstory was worth.

**Backstory in a thriller is delivered *at speed*, in motion, or not at all.**
