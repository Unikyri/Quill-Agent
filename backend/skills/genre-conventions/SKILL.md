# Genre Conventions

A genre is a **contract**. The reader picked this book off the shelf because they wanted a specific
experience, and they are owed it.

This is not a constraint on art. It is the reason the reader is here. A romance in which the couple
does not end up together has not been daring — it has broken its promise, and its readers will say
so, loudly and in public. A mystery whose solution was not fairly available to the reader has not
been clever; it has cheated.

Your job is to know what this book promised, and to notice when it is about to break that promise
by accident.

## How to use this skill

**Do not load every reference file. Load only the ones matching the universe's genre tags.**

A `horror + gothic` universe loads two files. A `romantasy` universe loads `romantasy.md` — and
likely `romance.md` and `fantasy.md`, since it inherits from both. Loading all twenty would defeat
the entire purpose of this design and flood the context with conventions that do not apply.

References live in `references/`, one per genre tag:

```
fantasy · epic-fantasy · urban-fantasy · romantasy
science-fiction · space-opera · dystopian · horror
gothic · paranormal · romance · mystery
cozy-mystery · thriller · crime · historical
literary · adventure · young-adult · coming-of-age
```

### When a universe carries multiple tags

Genres **combine**, and the combination is usually the point of the book. When tags conflict —
and they will, because a `horror + cozy-mystery` universe is pulling in two directions on purpose —
**the tension between them is where the book lives.** Do not resolve it for the writer. Name it,
and let them steer.

The one thing you must not do is enforce one genre's rules against a book that is deliberately
braiding two.

## What you look for

**Broken promises.** The convention the genre *requires*, about to be violated by accident rather
than by design. This is the finding that matters most.

**Missing beats.** The moment readers of this genre are waiting for, and which has not arrived.

**Exhausted tropes.** The convention that has been used so often it now reads as parody. Genre
readers are the most well-read audience in fiction — they have seen every version of this, and they
will be ahead of the book if it is not careful.

**Convention deployed with no awareness.** There is a large difference between subverting a trope
and simply not knowing it exists. The first delights genre readers. The second bores them.

## How to write a note

Name the convention. Say what readers of this genre expect at this moment and why. Then say whether
the passage is *subverting* it (deliberate, often good) or *missing* it (accidental, usually
costly) — and be honest that you may not be able to tell which, and ask.

## Calibration

**Convention is not obligation.** The greatest books in every genre broke its rules. But they broke
them **knowing they were rules**, and the breaking was the argument.

Your job is to make sure the writer knows what they are breaking. Not to stop them.
