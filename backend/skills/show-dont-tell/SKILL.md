# Show, Don't Tell

The most quoted rule in writing advice, and the most misapplied. Read the calibration section
first — it is the reason this skill exists in its current form.

## The failure you are looking for

**Emotion, announced.** *She was furious.* Two words, and the reader feels nothing, because they
have been *informed* of an emotion rather than made to experience one. Fury on the page is a
teacup set down half an inch too hard. It is a sentence that stops in the middle. It is the
politeness that arrives a beat too late.

**Character, asserted.** *He was the kind of man who never forgave a slight.* The reader has been
handed a conclusion and asked to take it on trust. Show him not forgiving one, and they will reach
that conclusion themselves — and believe it, because they got there on their own.

**Significance, underlined.** *It was a moment she would never forget.* If the moment works, the
reader knows. If it doesn't, saying so will not rescue it. This sentence is the writer's anxiety
leaking onto the page.

**The explained reaction.** A character does something, and the narration immediately explains why,
robbing the reader of the small pleasure of inference. Trust the reader. They are quicker than the
prose usually assumes.

## Now the part everyone forgets

**Telling is a legitimate tool, and often the right one.**

Summary is how a novel compresses time and skips what does not matter. *Three years passed.* *The
journey took a week and nothing happened.* A book that dramatises everything is not a vivid book —
it is a bloated one, four hundred thousand words long, in which the reader watches the protagonist
eat breakfast in real time.

Telling is right when:

- The material is **transitional** — getting from one scene that matters to the next.
- The material is **routine** and dramatising it would give it a weight it does not deserve.
- The **pace** demands it. Sometimes you need to cover ground fast, and scene-level rendering would
  kill the momentum.
- The **emotion is minor**. Not every feeling in a novel earns a rendered beat. *She was mildly
  annoyed* is fine. Reserve the machinery for the emotions that carry the book.
- The **voice is telling.** Some narrators — wry, distant, retrospective — tell by design, and the
  telling *is* the voice. Flagging that is not editing; it is flattening.

So the note is never "you told here". **The note is: *dramatising this would land harder, and here
is why.*** If dramatising it would not land harder, there is nothing to say.

## How to write a note

Point at the told sentence. Say what the reader is being handed instead of being given. Suggest the
*kind* of thing that would show it — an action, a detail, a line of dialogue, a physical response —
without writing it for them.

## Calibration — this is the whole skill

A skill that flags every instance of telling is a **nuisance**, and it will be switched off within
a week. The signal-to-noise ratio is what makes this useful or useless, and there is no middle
ground.

Flag the ones that matter: the big emotional beat that got summarised, the character trait
asserted instead of earned, the climactic moment reported from a distance. Leave the connective
tissue alone.

If the writer has rejected these notes repeatedly, they are telling you their voice is a telling
voice. **Believe them.**
