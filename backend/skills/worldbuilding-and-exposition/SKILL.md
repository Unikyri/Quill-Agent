# Worldbuilding & Exposition

A world must be **revealed at the speed of need**. Earlier is a lecture. Later is a cheat.

You have an advantage no human editor has: Quill's memory subsystem knows exactly what this world
has already established, and where. Use it. You can tell the difference between *"the reader does
not know this yet"* and *"the reader has been told this three times"* — and that distinction is
the entire skill.

## Too much, too early

**The infodump.** Three paragraphs of history, geography, or magical theory dropped into a scene
because the writer needed the reader to have it. The writer knows this world intimately — they
built it — and the desire to *share* it is enormous. It is also the most reliable way to make a
reader skim.

The reader does not need to understand the world. They need to understand **what is at stake in
this scene**. Everything else can wait, and waiting makes it land harder when it comes.

**"As you know, Bob."** Two characters explain to each other something they both already know, for
the reader's benefit. It is always visible, and it always breaks the fiction, because people do not
do this.

**The prologue that is a history lecture.** Occasionally this works. Usually it is the writer
warming up in public, and the actual book starts in chapter one.

**Explanation before consequence.** The mechanics of the magic system delivered before the reader
has seen the magic *matter*. Show the cost first; the reader will be hungry for the rules
afterwards. Curiosity is the appetite that makes exposition digestible, and it must be created
before it can be fed.

## Too little, too late

**Rules invented at the moment they are needed.** If the magic can suddenly do a thing the reader
had no way to anticipate, the victory it produces is worthless. This is *deus ex machina*, and it
is a failure of **setup**, not of imagination. The rule needed to exist — visibly — before it saved
anyone.

**Unearned stakes.** The reader cannot fear a threat whose consequences they do not understand. If
the ritual failing means "the Veil breaks", the reader needs to know what the Veil is and what
breaking it costs, *before* the ritual, or the climax is just words.

**Constraints never established.** A magic system without a cost is not a system; it is a wish.
What can it *not* do? What does it take from the user? The limits are where the story lives —
unlimited power is dramatically inert.

## The reveal test

For any piece of lore in the passage, ask three questions:

1. **Does the reader need this *now*?** Not soon. Now, to understand what is happening in front of
   them.
2. **Is it delivered through something happening**, or is the story paused to deliver it?
3. **Has it been established already?** Check the memory. Repeating established lore is a distinct
   and very common failure — the writer forgets what they already told us, and the reader feels
   condescended to.

## How to write a note

Point at the exposition. Say what the reader needed at that moment, and what they got instead.
Where lore is missing, name the *specific* thing that must be established earlier and say why the
later scene will not work without it.

## Calibration

**Some genres want the lecture.** Epic fantasy and hard science fiction readers arrive *hungry* for
world detail — it is a substantial part of why they came. Cozy fantasy and literary fiction readers
do not. Read the universe's genre tags before deciding how much is too much; the same paragraph can
be an infodump in one book and the best page in another.
